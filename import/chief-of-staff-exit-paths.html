Takeaway: The chief of staff role is designed to end, and the data shows most people leave it moving up rather than out, into operating leadership, functional executive seats, general management, or their own companies, which is exactly why it is worth taking in the first place.

The best thing about the chief of staff role is also the most misunderstood: it is not a destination. The job is intense, close to the top, and usually time-bound, and the people who take it tend to leave it for something bigger. If you are considering the role, the exit is not a risk to manage. It is the point. Where the role leads is a large part of what makes it worth the years of ambiguity and pressure.

This breaks down the actual exit paths, what the data says about them, and how to set up the move you want before you are in it.

The role is a springboard, and the numbers confirm it

McKinsey's study of roughly 250 chiefs of staff is the clearest evidence available. It found a median tenure of about 2.3 years, meaningfully shorter than the roughly 4.8 years of an average large-company CEO, which tells you the role is meant to be a chapter, not a career (McKinsey).

More striking is where people go. On average, chiefs of staff move up more than one level in their next role, a sizable jump given how short the typical tenure is. Two-thirds are promoted after the role, while about 18 percent use it as a launching pad for a lateral move, sometimes into a different industry or function. And four out of five take their next role in the same industry where they served, most often at the same company (McKinsey). The pattern is consistent: the role compresses experience and accelerates the climb.

Why the role sets up such strong exits

The reason the exits are so strong is structural. Ciampa's HBR description of the job explains it. A chief of staff spends the role acting as an air traffic controller for the leader's priorities, an integrator across functions, an honest broker on hard issues, and a confidant to a top executive. That is a front-row seat to how strategy is set, how decisions are made, and how an organization actually works at the top.

Few other roles at that career stage offer the same breadth and altitude at once. You leave with a map of the whole business, relationships across every function, and the trust of a senior leader. That combination is precisely what the next set of roles requires, which is why the transition tends to be a step up rather than a step sideways.

The common exit paths

1. Operating leadership, including COO

The most natural move is deeper into operations, up to and including chief operating officer. The role already involves running operating rhythms, integrating functions, and driving execution, which is the core of an operations leadership job. There is a well-documented path from chief of staff to COO, particularly for people who spent the role owning execution across the company (Ask a Chief of Staff). At a growing company, a chief of staff who helped build the operating machine is often the obvious person to run it.

2. Functional executive roles

Many chiefs of staff exit into a named functional seat, VP or head of strategy, business operations, corporate development, or a similar leadership role. This is common for people who, during the role, went deep on one area and found where they wanted to plant a flag. The chief of staff role doubles as an audition and a discovery process for which function fits.

3. General management and running a business unit

Because the role builds a whole-business perspective, some chiefs of staff move into general management, taking ownership of a product line, region, or business unit with real profit-and-loss responsibility. This path suits people who want to own outcomes end to end rather than orchestrate from the center.

4. Founding or joining a startup as an early operator

The role is often described as ideal preparation for entrepreneurship, because it hands you a front-row view of leadership, strategy, and execution at once. Some chiefs of staff leave to start their own companies. Others join early-stage startups as a first operator, a head of operations, or an early leader, applying the same range in a smaller, faster setting. The generalist breadth that the role builds maps unusually well onto early-stage company-building.

5. Back into the leadership team, one level up

The most common outcome in the McKinsey data is simply moving up within the same organization. Because two-thirds are promoted and four out of five stay in the same industry, often at the same company, the default exit is frequently an internal step up into the leadership team the chief of staff was already serving (McKinsey). The trust and context you built become the case for the promotion.

How to set up the exit you want

The exit is not automatic. The role can also end with a vague sideways move if you drift through it. A few deliberate choices tilt the odds.

  • Decide early where you want to land. The role touches everything, which means it can pull you in any direction. Pick a target, operations, a function, general management, or founding, in the first year so you can steer your projects toward it.
  • Go deep on something, not just wide. Breadth gets you the role. Depth in at least one area gets you the next one. Own a meaningful outcome end to end so you have a concrete result to point to, not just a list of things you coordinated.
  • Build the relationships as assets. The trust you earn across the leadership team is the raw material of your next role. Treat those relationships as long-term, not transactional.
  • Have the exit conversation before you are burned out. The role is demanding, and the median tenure is short for a reason. Raise your next move with your principal while you still have energy and leverage, ideally as a planned succession rather than a sudden departure. A principal who sponsors your next step is one of the most valuable outcomes of the whole role.
  • Name your transferable wins clearly. When you move on, translate the role into outcomes a hiring manager or promotion committee understands: the initiative you drove, the function you stood up, the decision process you fixed. Do not let the fuzzy title obscure concrete results.

FAQ

How long do people stay in a chief of staff role before leaving? The median tenure is about 2.3 years, notably shorter than a typical CEO's tenure, which reflects that the role is designed as a time-bound springboard (McKinsey).

What is the most common role after chief of staff? There is no single title, but the most common outcome is a promotion, often into operating or functional leadership within the same company. Two-thirds of chiefs of staff are promoted and most move up more than one level (McKinsey).

Can a chief of staff become a COO? Yes. It is one of the more direct paths, especially for chiefs of staff who spent the role owning execution and operating rhythm across the company (Ask a Chief of Staff).

Do chief of staff exits usually mean leaving the company? Not usually. About four out of five chiefs of staff take their next role in the same industry, frequently at the same company, so the common exit is an internal move up rather than a departure (McKinsey).

Is the chief of staff role a good move if I want to found a company someday? It is strong preparation. The role gives you a front-row view of strategy, leadership, and execution at once, which is why entrepreneurship and early-stage operating roles are a well-trodden exit path.
