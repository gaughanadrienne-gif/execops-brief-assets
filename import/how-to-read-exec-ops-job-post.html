Takeaway: A high salary does not make an exec-ops role a good one; the job post itself tells you whether the company understands what it is hiring, and that is what predicts whether the job will be worth having.

Executive-operations and Chief of Staff roles are among the harder job posts to read. The title is used loosely, the scope varies wildly, and the pay range can hide as much as it reveals. Two posts can both say "Chief of Staff, $160,000" and describe completely different jobs, one a genuine strategic partnership and one a re-titled admin role with a raise. This guide teaches you to tell them apart before you spend weeks interviewing.

First, read the post as a diagnostic

A job post is the company's clearest statement of how it thinks. For a role built on judgment and trust, how carefully the post is written is itself a signal. A vague, contradictory, or buzzword-heavy post usually reflects a company that has not decided what the role is, which means you would be walking into that ambiguity on day one.

Read the post twice. The first time, for what the job is. The second time, for what the company reveals about itself in describing it.

Green flags: signs of a real role

Strong exec-ops posts share a few traits. Look for them explicitly.

Concrete ownership, not vague support

The best posts name outcomes the person will own: "run the leadership team operating cadence," "own the OKR process end to end," "drive cross-functional initiatives to completion." The Chief of Staff Network's job description checklist makes the same point: strong descriptions emphasize strategic initiatives, decision support, and cross-functional leadership, with clearly spelled-out deliverables rather than "special projects as needed."

A clear reporting line

A real Chief of Staff role reports to the principal it supports, usually a CEO or a specific executive. If the post is coy about who you report to, or routes you through several layers, the scope is probably smaller than the title suggests.

Honest scope about the mix

Good posts admit that the role includes some operational and coordination work alongside the strategic parts, because every real Chief of Staff job does. A post that promises pure strategy with no execution is either naive or misleading.

A published, sensible pay range

A range that matches the market for the stage is a good sign. The 2025 Ask a Chief of Staff report (n=512) reports observed median base of $117,500 bootstrapped; $140,000 seed; $160,000 Series A; $170,000 Series B; $196,000 Series C; $197,500 Series D; $190,000 late-stage private; and $200,000 public. The Chief of Staff Network's 2025 report provides an overall cross-check with a $160,000 median and about $168,000 mean. A range that fits the relevant source population and company stage suggests the employer has calibrated the role.

Red flags: signs of a mislabeled or dead-end role

The "everything bagel" job description

If the post lists two dozen unrelated duties with no through-line, from calendar management to fundraising to office snacks, the company has not defined the role. The Chief of Staff Network's checklist frames this directly: a post heavy on calendar, travel, and admin tasks and light on strategic ownership is often a mislabeled executive assistant role or a dead-end "everything" position. Neither is what a $150k title should be.

Buzzwords standing in for scope

"Rockstar," "wear many hats," "ninja," "thrive in ambiguity" with nothing concrete underneath. Business press has flagged vague, hype-laden listings as a reliable warning sign for years; Inc.'s roundup of common job-listing red flags puts undefined expectations and buzzword filler near the top. Ambiguity in a post becomes ambiguity in your mandate.

Title inflation without the mandate

Startups increasingly attach senior titles to junior scope in place of competitive pay. Analysis of job postings has shown a sharp rise in senior-sounding titles on early-career roles over the past several years. A "Chief of Staff" who owns nothing and reports to a middle manager is a title, not a job. Read for authority, not adjectives.

A missing or implausible pay range

A role advertised at a senior level with no range, or a range far below the stage-appropriate band, signals either a budget problem or a scope problem. Where salary transparency is required by law, its absence is a flag in itself.

No definition of success

If the post cannot say what a good first year looks like, the company has not thought about what it needs. You will spend your first six months defining the job for them, without the authority to do it.

Reading the pay range specifically

A number in a post is a starting point, not a verdict. Interrogate it:

  • Base or total? A "$180,000" that bundles bonus and equity is a different offer than $180,000 base. Ask early.
  • Right for the stage? Match the range against the observed stage median. If you use a city or premium-job-market comparison, keep it separate; the evidence does not support one universal location or remote multiplier.
  • What is the equity story? At startups, the cash range is only part of the picture, but private-company equity is speculative. A lower base with equity may or may not beat a higher cash offer, and you cannot tell from a headline share count or percentage alone.

A quick scoring pass

Before you apply, run the post through five questions. Three or more clear yeses is a good sign.

  1. Can I name the top three things this person will own?
  2. Is the reporting line to the actual principal?
  3. Does the pay range fit the company's stage?
  4. Is there a definition of success or a clear first-year mandate?
  5. Does the writing suggest the company knows what a Chief of Staff is?

If you cannot answer the first question from the post, that is not a small gap. It is the whole job, undefined.

Frequently asked questions

Does a higher salary mean a better exec-ops role? No. Pay tells you what the company will spend, not whether the role is well-defined or set up to succeed. A clear mandate at $150,000 can be a far better job than a vague one at $180,000.

How do I know if a Chief of Staff post is really an EA role? Look at the balance of duties. Heavy calendar, travel, and admin with little strategic ownership usually signals a mislabeled executive assistant role, per the Chief of Staff Network's checklist.

Should I avoid posts without a salary range? Not automatically, but treat the absence as a question to resolve early. In markets with pay-transparency laws, a missing range is a stronger flag.

What is the single best signal of a good role? Concrete ownership. If the post names specific outcomes the person will own and who they report to, the company has thought the role through.

How should I read the equity portion of a startup post? As a separate, speculative line item. Ask for fully diluted ownership or the grant count and fully diluted shares; strike price; current 409A common-share value and date; preferred-round price and post-money valuation; vesting and cliff; post-termination exercise window; dilution and refresh policy; and change-of-control terms. If those inputs are missing, treat the equity as unvalued rather than converting it into offer value.
