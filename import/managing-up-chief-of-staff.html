Takeaway: Managing up means consciously working with your principal to get the best result for both of you, which starts with understanding how they operate and quietly covering the gaps rather than flattering them.

"Managing up" has an image problem. To a lot of people it sounds like political maneuvering, telling the boss what they want to hear, or angling for advantage. For a chief of staff it means almost the opposite. It is the disciplined practice of understanding the person you support well enough to make their time, attention, and decisions more effective. Done well, it is the core of the job.

The definitive treatment is still Gabarro and Kotter's Managing Your Boss, a Harvard Business Review classic. They define managing up as the process of consciously working with your superior to get the best possible results for you, your boss, and the company. Their central point is that the relationship is one of mutual dependence between two fallible people, and that leaving it to chance is a mistake that costs you both (HBR). For a chief of staff, whose entire value comes through another person, that dependence is the whole game.

Managing Up Is Not What People Think It Is

Start by clearing away the two common misreadings.

It is not flattery. Telling a principal what they want to hear makes you pleasant and useless. The operators principals actually rely on are the ones who will say the uncomfortable thing in private, precisely because they have earned the standing to be believed.

It is not manipulation. You are not steering someone toward your preferred outcome behind their back. You are making sure they have what they need to make good decisions, and removing the friction that keeps them from acting on them.

Gabarro and Kotter frame the real work as understanding both parties: your boss's goals, pressures, strengths, and blind spots, and your own reactions and tendencies, so that you can build a relationship that fits how you both actually operate. That is a practice, not a personality trait.

Understand Your Principal in Concrete Detail

You cannot make someone more effective if you do not know how they work. Vague impressions are not enough. Get specific, and where you can, ask rather than assume.

Working style and information diet

Some principals think out loud and want to talk through a problem. Others want a written brief and quiet time to form a view. Some read every word. Some want the recommendation in the first sentence and the reasoning underneath. If you send a three-page memo to someone who processes verbally, or corner a writer for an on-the-spot decision, you are creating friction and calling it communication.

Match the format to the person. This one adjustment, more than any other, determines whether your principal experiences you as a help or a tax.

Goals and pressures

Understand what your principal is actually accountable for and what is weighing on them this quarter, including the pressures coming from above them that they may not talk about openly. When you know what they are being measured on, you can prioritize your own work against it instead of guessing.

Strengths and gaps

Every leader has things they do brilliantly and things they consistently drop. Your job is not to fix their personality. It is to notice the recurring gap, the follow-through that slips, the detail that gets missed, the relationship that goes cold, and quietly cover it. Watkins makes a version of this point in The First 90 Days: complementing your boss's weaknesses is more useful than admiring their strengths (Harvard Business Review Press).

The Practices That Make a Principal More Effective

Understanding is the foundation. Here is what you do with it.

Protect their time and attention

Executive calendars fill with meetings that could have been a document. Recall that in one HBR survey, 65 percent of senior managers said meetings kept them from doing their own work (Perlow, Hadley & Eun). A chief of staff who manages up well treats the principal's calendar as a scarce, defensible resource: declining what does not need them, converting standing meetings into written updates where possible, and guarding blocks of uninterrupted time for the thinking only they can do.

Bring decisions, not problems

The fastest way to become indispensable is to stop bringing your principal open-ended problems and start bringing them framed decisions. Instead of "here is an issue, what do you want to do," bring the issue, the two or three real options, the tradeoffs, and your recommendation. Now they can decide in two minutes instead of twenty, and they can override you when they see something you missed. This also trains your own judgment, because you have to actually take a position.

Give them a clear line of sight

Principals lose confidence when they feel out of the loop on things they are accountable for. A short, regular brief, what moved this week, what needs your attention, what is coming, gives them a reliable line of sight without pulling them into every detail. It also concentrates the small number of things that genuinely need their input, so they are not managing by interruption.

No surprises

Few things erode a principal's trust faster than being blindsided, especially in front of others. If something is going sideways, they should hear it from you early and in private, while there is still time to act. A chief of staff who reliably surfaces bad news early becomes someone the principal can stop worrying about, which is worth more than any individual project.

Tell them the truth

Because you sit close to the principal and often outside the formal hierarchy, you may be one of the few people willing to give them an unvarnished read. Use that carefully and privately, but use it. A principal surrounded by agreement makes worse decisions. The value of a trusted operator is partly that they will say "I think you are wrong about this, and here is why" when no one else will.

Adapt to the Principal You Have, Not the One You Wish For

A common mistake is to manage every principal the same way. The leader who wants tight control over decisions needs a different kind of support than the one who wants to delegate broadly. The detail-oriented principal and the big-picture principal need different briefs. Gabarro and Kotter are explicit that there is no generic formula, because the whole point is to fit the relationship to the two specific people in it.

Pay attention to how your principal reacts, and adjust. If a certain kind of update always prompts follow-up questions, you are leaving something out. If they keep re-deciding things you thought were settled, your framing is not landing. Managing up is a feedback loop, not a fixed procedure.

Where the Line Is

Managing up has a healthy version and an unhealthy one. The healthy version makes the principal more effective and the organization better served. The unhealthy version optimizes for the principal's comfort or your own standing at the expense of the truth.

Two guardrails keep you on the right side. First, are you making them more effective, or just more comfortable? Comfort that comes from hiding problems is a debt that comes due. Second, would the practice survive daylight? If you would not be willing to have your principal see exactly how you are handling something, it is not managing up, it is maneuvering.

FAQ

Is managing up just corporate politics? No. Politics optimizes for your own advantage. Managing up, as Gabarro and Kotter define it, means working consciously with your principal to get the best result for both of you and the organization. The test is whether the truth is being served. If you are hiding problems or flattering, you have crossed into something else.

How is managing up different from just doing what my boss says? Following orders is passive. Managing up is active. It means understanding your principal well enough to anticipate what they need, frame decisions for them, protect their time, and tell them hard truths, sometimes including pushing back when you think they are wrong.

What if my principal does not want to be managed up? You are not managing them, you are adapting to them. Most of the practice is invisible: matching your communication to their style, bringing them clean decisions, guarding their calendar, and flagging problems early. None of that requires their buy-in. It just makes their work easier, which they tend to notice.

How do I push back on a principal without damaging the relationship? Do it in private, frame it around their goals rather than your preference, and bring reasoning, not just objection. Trust is what buys you the standing to disagree, so it is worth building deliberately before you need to spend it.

How do I learn my principal's working style quickly? Ask directly where you can: how they like to receive information, what they want to be looped in on, and what they would rather have handled without them. Then watch the pattern of their reactions and adjust. It is a feedback loop, and the first 90 days are when you calibrate it.
