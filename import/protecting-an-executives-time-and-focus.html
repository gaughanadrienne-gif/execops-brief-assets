Takeaway: An executive's attention is the scarcest and most leveraged resource in the organization, and the operator's job is to defend it deliberately, protecting blocks of uninterrupted focus, filtering what reaches them, and reducing the switching costs that quietly degrade every decision they make.

Ask a chief of staff what they protect and most will say the calendar. That is close, but it undersells the point. You are not protecting time as a block of hours. You are protecting attention, which is a different and more fragile thing. An executive with a full calendar and a fractured mind produces worse decisions than one with fewer meetings and the room to think. The research on how attention breaks down is clear enough that an operator can build a real practice on it.

Why attention, not time, is the resource

The reason to obsess over an executive's focus is leverage. Their decisions ripple across the whole organization, so a poorly considered call at the top costs far more than a delayed email. And the evidence says that fragmented days produce exactly those poorly considered calls.

Start with the cost of interruption. Gloria Mark and colleagues at the University of California, Irvine, in a widely cited study, found that after an interruption it takes people an average of about 23 minutes to return to the original task (UC Irvine). Now picture an executive whose day is a run of back-to-back meetings with no gaps. Every transition carries a version of that reset cost, and the thinking that requires sustained attention never gets a clean run.

Then there is attention residue. Sophie Leroy's research describes what happens when people switch between tasks: a part of the mind stays stuck on the previous task, and that lingering residue degrades performance on the next one (Leroy, 2009). An executive who goes from a tense personnel conversation straight into a strategy review carries residue from the first into the second. They are physically present and cognitively half-gone. Protecting focus is partly about giving them enough separation that each thing gets a whole mind.

Finally, meetings themselves are a documented drain on senior people. In a Harvard Business Review survey, 65 percent of senior managers said meetings kept them from completing their own work, and 71 percent said meetings are unproductive and inefficient (Perlow, Hadley & Eun). The executive you support is very likely in that majority, whether or not they have said so.

Treat the calendar as a defended resource

Most executive calendars are built by accretion. Requests come in, they get accepted, and the day fills with whatever asked first rather than whatever matters most. The operator's first job is to reverse that default so the calendar reflects priorities rather than the order of arrival.

Protect focus blocks before anything else. Put the executive's most important thinking on the calendar as real, defended time, ideally in the part of the day when they are sharpest, and treat it as roughly as immovable as an external board meeting. The reason to schedule it rather than hope for it is that unscheduled time is the first thing meetings colonize. If the strategy work only happens in the gaps, it does not happen.

Batch by type. Grouping similar meetings together, all one-on-ones in one stretch, all reviews in another, reduces the number of costly context switches. Given the residue and reset costs above, a day organized into a few modes is meaningfully more productive than a day that alternates between unrelated tasks every thirty minutes, even with the same total meeting load.

Build in recovery. Back-to-back scheduling looks efficient and is not, because it guarantees the executive carries residue from each meeting into the next and never has a minute to prepare. Default meetings to 25 or 50 minutes instead of 30 or 60, so there is a built-in gap. The buffer is where preparation and decompression live.

Audit the recurring load. Standing meetings are where calendars quietly rot. Every one of them should have to justify its continued existence. Ask periodically which recurring meetings could be shorter, less frequent, delegated, or replaced by a written update. The 65 percent figure above is a standing invitation to cut.

Filter what reaches them

Defending the calendar addresses time. Defending attention also means controlling the flow of requests, decisions, and information, so that only what genuinely needs the executive reaches them. This is where an operator adds leverage that a scheduling tool cannot.

The core discipline is triage. Most of what people want to bring to an executive falls into one of a few buckets: things you can handle yourself, things someone else should own, things that can wait for a regular batch, and the small number of things that genuinely need the principal now. An operator who sorts incoming requests into those buckets, and who is trusted to sort them well, can absorb or redirect the majority before it ever reaches the executive's desk.

Two habits make this work without turning you into a bottleneck. First, bring decisions rather than open questions. When something does need the executive, hand them the framed choice, the options, and your recommendation, so the decision takes two minutes instead of twenty. Second, batch the non-urgent. Instead of interrupting the principal eight times a day, hold a short list and clear it in one focused pass. Both practices convert a stream of small interruptions, each with its own reset cost, into a couple of contained sessions.

Protect their energy, not just their hours

Attention is also physical. An executive who is scheduled from 8 a.m. to 7 p.m. with no breaks, no meals, and no thinking time is not being well served, however full the calendar looks. Part of protecting focus is protecting the conditions that make focus possible: a real gap for lunch, travel time that is actually blocked, and an honest limit on how many high-stakes conversations you stack into one day. There is a point past which adding another important meeting subtracts from every meeting around it. A good operator learns where that line is for their specific principal and holds it.

The bottleneck trap

There is a failure mode to name. An operator who filters aggressively can become a wall, the person everyone has to get through, which slows the whole organization and isolates the executive from information they need. The goal is to be a filter, not a gate. A filter removes noise and lets signal through. A gate blocks by default and makes everyone wait.

The way to stay on the right side of that line is judgment about what the executive actually wants to see. Some principals want to be looped into far more than others. The best protection is calibrated to the person, learned by watching which items they later say they wish they had known about and which ones they say you should simply have handled. That feedback loop, run over the first few months, is what turns a well-meaning filter into a trusted one.

FAQ

Isn't protecting an executive's time just managing their calendar? The calendar is the visible part. The deeper job is protecting their attention: reducing context switching, filtering what reaches them, and preserving blocks of uninterrupted focus for the decisions only they can make. A full calendar and a fractured mind is not a win.

How do I protect focus time when everyone wants a meeting? Schedule the focus blocks as defended commitments before the day fills, batch similar meetings together, and make standing meetings justify themselves regularly. Given that after an interruption it takes about 23 minutes to fully refocus (UC Irvine), protected blocks are not a luxury; they are where real work happens.

How do I filter requests without becoming a bottleneck? Triage into what you can handle, what someone else should own, what can wait for a batch, and what truly needs the principal now. Bring framed decisions rather than open questions, and batch the non-urgent so you interrupt once instead of eight times. The aim is to be a filter that passes signal, not a gate that blocks by default.

Why does back-to-back scheduling hurt more than it helps? Because switching tasks leaves attention residue that degrades the next task (Leroy, 2009), and no-gap days leave no room to prepare or recover. Shorter default meeting lengths that build in a buffer usually produce better decisions than a wall-to-wall calendar.

How do I know how much to protect versus how much to let through? Calibrate to your specific principal. Watch which items they later wish they had seen and which they say you should have handled, and adjust. Protection that is right for one executive is over- or under-filtering for another.
