Takeaway: A company needs a chief of staff when a leader's time and the organization's decision-making have become the bottleneck, not when the founder simply wants help, and the clearest signals are lost productivity, slow decisions, and too much of the leader's week disappearing into follow-up.

The chief of staff role gets added for the wrong reasons more often than people admit. A leader is overwhelmed, hiring one feels like a fix, and a title appears without a clear problem behind it. Six months later the role is fuzzy, the fit is off, and nobody is sure what changed. The role works when it answers a specific need and struggles when it is a vague reaction to overload.

This is a timing guide: the concrete signals that a company is ready for the role, how to size it correctly, and the cases where a chief of staff is the wrong answer.

The role solves a bottleneck, not a workload

Start with what the role is for. A chief of staff exists to make one leader and their leadership team more effective by owning the priorities, information flow, and follow-through that otherwise fall through the cracks. That is a different thing from giving a busy executive more hands. The right question is not "is the leader busy," because every leader is busy. The right question is "has the leader, or the organization's decision-making, become the constraint on progress."

Dan Ciampa framed the readiness question well in Harvard Business Review, drawing on Liza Wright, a former White House director of presidential personnel who later helped fill chief of staff roles. She looks for three signs that a leader would benefit from adding one: concern about the leader's productivity, poor information flow that results in slow decision-making, and too much of the leader's time consumed by back-and-forth and follow-up (HBR). Those three are the cleanest test available.

The signals that justify the role

Translate those signs into things you can actually observe inside a company.

1. Decisions are getting stuck

Important calls sit unmade because the information needed is scattered, the right people are not in the room at the same time, or nobody owns the follow-through. When the leadership team keeps relitigating decisions that were supposedly already made, that is a coordination failure a chief of staff is built to fix.

2. The leader is the bottleneck on everything

Progress across multiple functions routes through one person, and that person cannot keep up. The signal is not that the leader is tired. It is that work stalls waiting on the leader's attention, and the organization slows to the speed of a single calendar.

3. Cross-functional work keeps falling apart

Initiatives that span two or more departments stall because no single executive owns the seam between them. Each function does its part and the whole still fails. This is the integrator problem, and it grows worse as a company adds functions and headcount.

4. The leadership operating rhythm is broken

There is no reliable cadence for how the leadership team meets, decides, and tracks. Meetings sprawl, agendas are improvised, decisions are not captured, and the same topics resurface. A functioning operating rhythm has to be owned by someone, and it is a natural home for the role.

5. Strategy is set but not landing

The leadership team agrees on direction, then nothing changes on the ground because the translation from strategy to action, and the communication that carries it, has no owner. A good decision with no follow-through is indistinguishable from no decision.

When several of these are true at once, the case is strong. When only one is, look harder at whether a narrower fix would do.

Company stage and size matter, but less than you think

There is no headcount threshold that automatically calls for a chief of staff. The role shows up at ten-person startups and at Fortune 100 companies, because it tracks the complexity a specific leader faces rather than the size of the org chart.

That said, two moments commonly trigger genuine need. The first is rapid scaling, when a company is adding people and functions faster than its coordination can keep up, and the founder becomes the constraint. The second is a period of high complexity for the leader specifically, such as a new executive driving a strategy shift or transformation while still running the business. Ciampa's levels of the role map to exactly this (HBR): a leader in a stable environment who mainly needs help with time and information flow needs a lighter version, while a leader facing a complex change in a volatile environment needs a more senior, strategic one.

Size the role before you fill it

Getting the timing right is only half the job. The other half is scoping the role to the actual need, because a mis-sized chief of staff fails regardless of talent.

If the need is mostly time management, information flow, and lifting an overloaded leader, a lighter, more operational version works, and an experienced executive assistant ready for more can sometimes grow into it. If the need is driving strategy, integrating functions, and acting as an honest broker across a complex organization, the role needs real seniority and business fluency to be credible with executives. McKinsey's study of roughly 250 chiefs of staff found the average person entering the role brought just over 12 years of experience and typically came from a midlevel leadership position (McKinsey). The strategic version is not an entry-level hire, and pretending it is sets everyone up to fail.

When a chief of staff is the wrong answer

The role is a poor fit in several common situations.

  • The real gap is a functional leader. If the company actually needs someone to own operations, finance, or product with formal authority, hire that person. A chief of staff has borrowed authority, not a functional mandate, and cannot substitute for a missing COO or VP.
  • The leader will not delegate. The role only works if the principal genuinely shares information, context, and trust. A leader who wants help but will not let go turns the role into an expensive assistant with an inflated title.
  • The problem is a lack of strategy, not a lack of coordination. A chief of staff can execute and integrate around a clear direction. They cannot supply the direction the leader has not set.
  • It is a title in place of a promotion. Handing someone the label without the access, scope, and seat that define the role creates a coordinator job dressed up as something more, which helps no one.

FAQ

How big does a company need to be to have a chief of staff? There is no fixed size. The role tracks the complexity a leader faces, not headcount, and it appears at small startups and large enterprises alike (HBR).

What is the difference between needing a chief of staff and needing a COO? A COO owns operations with formal authority and a permanent seat. A chief of staff supports a leader through influence and is usually time-bound. If the gap is functional ownership, you need the COO. If the gap is coordination, decision flow, and follow-through around the leader, you need the chief of staff.

Can an executive assistant grow into a chief of staff role instead of hiring one? Sometimes, when the need is mostly operational, an experienced assistant who is ready for more responsibility can grow into a lighter version of the role (HBR). The strategic version usually calls for more seniority and business fluency.

What happens if you hire a chief of staff too early? You get an undefined role and a mismatch. Without a clear bottleneck to solve, the position drifts, the fit feels wrong, and it often ends within a year. Wait for a genuine, nameable need.

How senior should a chief of staff be? It depends on the version. McKinsey found the average person entering the role brought just over 12 years of experience, which reflects how much credibility with executives the strategic version requires (McKinsey).
