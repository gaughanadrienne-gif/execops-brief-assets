Takeaway: Your first 90 days as a chief of staff are for diagnosing the situation, earning trust, and delivering one or two visible wins, not for reorganizing everything before you understand why it works the way it does.

The chief of staff role is unusual in that it rarely comes with a job description you can execute against on day one. You are hired to make a principal and a leadership team more effective, but what that means depends entirely on the person, the company, and the moment. That ambiguity is why the first 90 days matter so much. The habits, relationships, and reputation you build in the first three months tend to define how much leverage you have for the next two years.

The most durable framework for this period is Michael Watkins's The First 90 Days, the standard reference for leadership transitions with more than a million copies in print (Harvard Business Review Press). The plan below adapts its core ideas to the specific realities of the chief of staff role.

First, Diagnose the Situation You Are Actually In

Watkins's central insight is that there is no single right way to start a new role, because new roles come in different shapes. His STARS model names five of them: start-up, turnaround, accelerated growth, realignment, and sustaining success. Each calls for a different early strategy, and the fastest way to fail is to apply a turnaround playbook to a healthy team, or a caretaker's touch to a business that is on fire (Watkins, HBR).

For a chief of staff, the question is usually some version of these:

  • Is this a new function or an existing one? If you are the first chief of staff the company has had, you are in a start-up situation and you are building the role from scratch. If you are replacing someone, you have inherited expectations, relationships, and possibly baggage.
  • Is the leadership team healthy or in trouble? A team that is aligned and executing needs a very different kind of help than one that is fighting or drifting.
  • Why does the principal want this role filled now? The real answer, which you have to draw out carefully, tells you what problem you are really being hired to solve.

Spend your first days getting clear on which situation you are in. It changes everything that follows.

Days 1 to 30: Learn Before You Act

The strongest temptation in a new chief of staff role is to prove your worth immediately by fixing something visible. Resist it. In the first month your job is to build an accurate map of the organization, and you cannot do that while you are busy rearranging it.

Run a structured listening tour

Set up 30-minute conversations with every member of the leadership team and a selection of people one and two levels down. Ask the same core questions of everyone so you can compare answers:

  • What is working well right now that we should protect?
  • What is the biggest thing getting in the team's way?
  • If you had my role, what is the first thing you would look at?
  • How do decisions actually get made here?

Take careful notes and watch for patterns. When five people independently name the same broken process, you have found real work. When they each name something different, the problem is usually alignment itself.

Understand your principal in detail

The chief of staff relationship rises or falls on how well you understand the person you support. In the classic HBR article Managing Your Boss, Gabarro and Kotter frame this as a relationship of mutual dependence and argue that you need a clear read on your principal's strengths, weaknesses, working style, and needs before you can be genuinely useful (HBR).

Get specific and early. How does the principal prefer to consume information, in writing or in conversation? What do they want to be looped in on, and what do they want handled without them? Where do they lose the most time? What does a good week look like from their seat? Where possible, ask these questions directly rather than guessing.

Map how the machine runs

Document the current operating rhythm as it exists, not as it should be. What meetings happen, who attends, and what are they for? How does the company plan and track goals? Where do decisions get made and recorded, if they get recorded at all? You are building the baseline you will improve later, and you will spot the gaps just by writing it down.

Days 31 to 60: Earn Trust and Pick Your First Win

By the second month you have a map. Now you convert learning into a small amount of credible action.

Choose one or two early wins, carefully

Watkins emphasizes securing early wins that build momentum and credibility, but the choice matters more than the speed. For a chief of staff, the best first win is usually a visible improvement to something the leadership team already feels the pain of: a meeting that wastes everyone's time, a planning process that produces no follow-through, a decision that keeps getting relitigated because no one wrote it down.

Pick something you can genuinely improve within a few weeks, that helps the whole team rather than just the principal, and that does not require you to have political capital you have not earned yet. Tightening the weekly leadership meeting is a common and safe first project, because everyone benefits and the improvement is obvious.

Avoid the trap of the ambitious reorganization. Reworking the entire operating model in month two, before you fully understand why the current one exists, is how new chiefs of staff burn trust fast.

Establish your working relationship with the principal

This is the month to move from observing your principal to actively supporting them. Set up a recurring one-on-one if you do not have one. Start producing a short weekly brief that surfaces what needs their attention. Begin taking small things off their plate and closing the loop reliably, so they learn that anything handed to you comes back done.

Reliability compounds. Every commitment you keep in these weeks makes the next, larger delegation possible.

Build relationships across the leadership team

A chief of staff has no formal authority over the executives they work with. Influence comes entirely from trust and usefulness. Use this month to become genuinely helpful to each leader individually, so that when you later need to hold the team to a process, you are enforcing it as a trusted partner rather than as the principal's enforcer.

Days 61 to 90: Install the System

By the final month you have earned enough trust to put durable structure in place. This is where you shift from fixing individual problems to building systems that prevent them.

Set up the operating cadence

Lock in the rhythm the leadership team will run on: the weekly tactical meeting, the longer strategic sessions, the planning and review cycle, and how goals are tracked between them. If any of these did not exist or ran poorly, this is when you formalize them.

Stand up a single source of truth

Start a decision log and a clear home for operating documents. Even a simple, well-maintained record of what was decided, by whom, and why, saves the team from relitigating settled questions and gives new people a way to get up to speed. This becomes one of the most quietly valuable things you own.

Agree on how you will be measured

Close the first 90 days with an explicit conversation with your principal about what success looks like over the next two quarters. The role is broad enough that you and the principal can easily hold different definitions of a good job. Naming a few concrete priorities and how you will both know they are on track turns an ambiguous role into a plan.

A 90-Day Plan on One Page

  • Days 1 to 30: Diagnose the situation. Listen widely, understand the principal, map the operating rhythm. Change little.
  • Days 31 to 60: Deliver one or two visible wins. Build the working relationship with the principal. Earn trust across the leadership team.
  • Days 61 to 90: Install the system. Set the operating cadence, stand up a single source of truth, and agree on how your success will be measured.

FAQ

Should I make changes in my first week as chief of staff? Only small, obviously helpful ones. The first weeks are for understanding why things work the way they do. Large changes made before you have that context tend to break things you did not know were load-bearing and cost you trust you have not yet earned.

What if the company has never had a chief of staff before? You are in a start-up situation, in Watkins's terms, which means part of your first 90 days is defining the role itself. Be explicit with your principal about what you will and will not own, and expect to renegotiate the scope as both of you learn what actually helps.

How do I show value early without overstepping? Pick a problem the whole leadership team already feels, improve it visibly within a few weeks, and give credit generously. Improving the weekly leadership meeting or a broken planning process are common early wins because they help everyone and do not require political capital you have not built yet.

How much time should I spend with my principal in the first month? Enough to understand their working style, priorities, and pressures in real detail, which usually means a standing one-on-one plus regular informal contact. The goal is to learn how they think and where they lose time, so you can start removing friction rather than adding to it.
