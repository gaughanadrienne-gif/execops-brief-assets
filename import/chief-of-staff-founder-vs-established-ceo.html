Takeaway: The chief of staff title stays the same, but the job splits into two very different roles depending on the principal: a founder chief of staff fills gaps and builds what does not exist yet, while a chief of staff to an established CEO orchestrates and improves a machine that is already running.

Two people can hold the title chief of staff, work the same hours, and do almost nothing alike. The single biggest variable is not the industry or the company size. It is who you serve and what stage that leader's organization is in. Working for a founder at a young company and working for a seasoned CEO at an established one pull the role in opposite directions.

This breaks down where the two versions diverge, what each demands, and how to tell which one a given job actually is before you take it.

Why the principal defines the role

The chief of staff role has no fixed job description. Dan Ciampa made this point in Harvard Business Review: the role is shaped by the specific needs of the leader it serves, and he lays out different levels of the role depending on how stable or complex the leader's situation is. A leader in a stable environment who mainly needs help with time and information flow needs a very different chief of staff than a leader driving a new strategy through a volatile situation.

Founders and established CEOs sit at opposite ends of that spectrum more often than not. The founder is usually building the plane while flying it. The established CEO is usually flying a plane that already exists and wants it to fly better.

The founder chief of staff: building what is not there yet

Working for a founder, especially at an early or scaling company, is defined by absence. The functions, processes, and hires that a mature company takes for granted often do not exist. The chief of staff frequently becomes the person who fills those gaps until the company can afford or justify a dedicated hire.

Early-stage venture investors describe this directly. As Airtree Ventures notes in its guide to the startup chief of staff, in the early stages the role often means standing up and running functions the company does not yet have a full-time owner for, whether that is sales, recruiting, operations, or finance support (Airtree). The job is broad, fluid, and changes quarter to quarter as the company grows into functions that eventually replace parts of your role.

What the founder version demands

  • Comfort with ambiguity. There is no playbook, and often no process to inherit. You build the process, then hand it off.
  • Range over depth. In a single week you might touch fundraising prep, a hiring plan, a customer escalation, and an operating cadence that does not exist yet. You need to be functional across all of them, not expert in any one.
  • Speed and adaptability. Priorities can shift on short notice as the company learns what works. A plan set on Monday can be obsolete by Friday, and you have to move with that.
  • Willingness to make yourself redundant. Much of the founder chief of staff job is building something well enough that a specialist can take it over. Doing that repeatedly is the point, not a threat.

The upside is exposure. The role is often described as a 12 to 24 month window with a front-row seat to every part of the business, which is why it is such a strong launch pad into operating and general management roles.

The established-CEO chief of staff: orchestrating a running machine

Working for an established CEO at a mature company is a different job. The functions exist. There are heads of sales, finance, product, and operations, each with their own teams, budgets, and agendas. The problem is rarely that something is missing. The problem is coordination, information flow, and follow-through across a large and siloed organization.

Here the chief of staff spends more time on the roles Ciampa emphasizes for complex organizations: the air traffic controller managing what reaches the leader, the integrator connecting work streams that would otherwise stay siloed, and the honest broker who can give the CEO an unbiased read across functions that each have turf to defend (HBR). The work is less about building missing functions and more about making existing ones cohere.

What the established version demands

  • Organizational navigation. You are working across established leaders with real authority and real interests. Influence without direct authority is the core skill, because you cannot order senior executives around.
  • Depth on the operating rhythm. Running the cadence of leadership meetings, the planning cycle, and the metrics reviews well is a large part of the value.
  • Political judgment. In a mature organization, how a decision is made and communicated matters as much as the decision itself. Missteps are costly and visible.
  • Discipline over improvisation. Success looks like a well-run system, not heroic gap-filling. The wins are quieter and more structural.

The shared core, and the honest trade-offs

Both versions share the same center of gravity. In each case the chief of staff exists to make one leader and their priorities more effective, runs on borrowed authority and earned trust rather than a formal mandate, and tends to be a time-bound springboard rather than a permanent seat. McKinsey's study of roughly 250 chiefs of staff found a median tenure of about 2.3 years and that most people move up more than one level afterward, which holds across company types (McKinsey).

The trade-offs cut in opposite directions. The founder role offers unmatched range, speed, and equity upside, at the cost of chaos, thin support, and a role that can feel like it is being rebuilt constantly. The established-CEO role offers structure, resources, and a clearer path to a named senior title, at the cost of slower movement, more politics, and less blank-canvas creativity. Neither is better. They suit different people and different points in a career.

One more practical note on how you get hired for each. McKinsey found that nearly two-thirds of chiefs of staff are appointed from within, and at companies with 50,000 or more employees, 80 percent are internal hires (McKinsey). Established-CEO roles lean heavily on institutional knowledge and internal trust, so they are harder to break into from outside. Founder roles are more often open to strong outside operators who can start building immediately.

How to tell which job you are actually interviewing for

Before you accept a chief of staff role, diagnose the version. Ask the principal what the first six months would fix. If the answer is a list of things that do not exist yet, functions to stand up, hires to make, processes to build, you are looking at the founder version. If the answer is about coordination, decision speed, and getting the leadership team rowing together, you are looking at the established version. Then ask yourself honestly which kind of work energizes you, because the title will not tell you, and the mismatch is the most common reason these roles end badly.

FAQ

Is a founder chief of staff more strategic than one for an established CEO? Not necessarily. The founder version is broader and more improvisational, while the established version is more orchestration-heavy. Both involve strategy, but the founder role blends it with hands-on gap-filling and the established role blends it with cross-functional coordination.

Which pays more, a founder or an established-CEO chief of staff? It depends on the company. Established companies often offer higher and more predictable cash compensation, while founder roles may offer lower cash but meaningful equity. Evaluate the whole package rather than the base alone.

Is it easier to get a chief of staff job at a startup? Often yes, from the outside. Established-CEO roles are frequently filled internally because they lean on institutional knowledge, with up to 80 percent internal hires at the largest companies (McKinsey). Founder roles are more open to outside operators.

Does the chief of staff role end differently in each case? The exit differs in flavor, not in fact. Both are usually time-bound. Founder chiefs of staff often move into an operating or functional leadership role they helped build, while established-company chiefs of staff often move up into a named senior seat within the same organization.
