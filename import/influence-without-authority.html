Takeaway: A chief of staff almost never has formal authority over the people whose cooperation they need, so the job runs on influence: understanding what others value, trading it fairly, and building the kind of credibility that makes a request easy to say yes to.

The authority gap in the role

The chief of staff sits close to the top of an organization but rarely holds a line of command. You do not sign the performance reviews of the department heads you coordinate. You do not control their budgets. You cannot order the head of engineering to move a deadline or tell the head of sales to change a forecast. Yet the work only counts if those people act.

This is the central tension of the role. The title carries proximity to power without carrying power itself. New operators often mistake the proximity for the power, lean on it, and watch their requests stall. The people who thrive learn quickly that the real tool is influence, and that influence is a skill with a structure, not a personality trait you either have or lack.

Why authority was never the point

It helps to see that operating through influence is not a workaround for a weak role. It is how most consequential work gets done in any organization above a certain size. Cross-functional projects, standards, planning cycles, and change efforts all depend on people cooperating outside their reporting lines. The chief of staff is simply a concentrated version of a challenge every senior contributor faces.

Two bodies of work explain how it functions. The first is Robert Cialdini's research on persuasion. The second is the exchange model developed by Allan Cohen and David Bradford in their book Influence Without Authority. Together they cover most of what an operator needs.

The reciprocity engine

Cohen and Bradford build their model on what they call the Law of Reciprocity, the widely observed tendency for people to want to repay what others do for them. Their framing is practical rather than moralistic. If you want cooperation, you look for something the other person values that you can offer, and you trade it for what you need to move the work forward (Cohen and Bradford, The Influence Model).

They use the metaphor of currencies to describe what people value. Not money, but the things that make a colleague's day better or their goals more reachable. Their categories include inspiration-related currencies (a sense of vision or excellence), task-related currencies (resources, information, help with a project), position-related currencies (recognition, visibility, contacts), relationship-related currencies (belonging, support), and personal currencies (gratitude, a sense of being valued or effective) (Currencies of Exchange).

For a chief of staff, this reframes the entire influence problem. When a department head resists a request, the useful question is not "how do I make them comply?" It is "what does this person care about, and what can I offer that helps them get it?" Often the answer is currency you already control: air time with the principal, cover on a competing priority, a cleaner decision process, credit for a win, or simply information they did not have.

Working the model in practice

Cohen and Bradford lay out a repeatable process. Assume the other person is a potential ally rather than an obstacle. Get clear on your own goals and priorities so you know what you actually need. Understand the other person's world, including the pressures they face and the metrics they are judged on. Identify the currencies that matter to them and the ones you can offer. Tend to the relationship along the way. Then influence through give and take.

The step operators skip most often is understanding the other person's world. It is tempting to walk in with a request framed around your principal's needs. The request lands far better when it is framed around what the other person is trying to accomplish. A budget freeze looks different to a leader worried about missing a hiring plan than it does from the finance seat, and the operator who names that pressure earns cooperation the one who ignores it will not.

Cialdini's principles, applied to the operator's seat

Cialdini's work identifies principles that reliably make a request more persuasive. He documented six in his book Influence and later added a seventh, unity, in Pre-Suasion (Influence at Work, 7 Principles of Persuasion). Several map directly onto the chief of staff role.

Reciprocity. People tend to return favors. An operator who is consistently useful to colleagues, who unblocks them, shares context, and makes them look good, builds a balance others want to repay. This is not manipulation if the help is genuine. It is the ordinary economy of a functioning team.

Authority. Cialdini's authority principle is about perceived expertise and trustworthiness, not job title. People defer to those they see as credible and knowledgeable in the relevant domain (Influence at Work). A chief of staff cannot command, but can become the person who clearly understands how the company runs, what the priorities are, and why a decision was made. That earned credibility is a source of influence that no org chart confers.

Liking. People say yes more readily to those they like and feel similar to. For an operator, this is the case for treating relationships as part of the job rather than a distraction from it. The coffee, the hallway question about a colleague's project, the willingness to listen before asking, all of it compounds.

Consistency and social proof. People like to act in line with prior commitments and with what their peers are doing. An operator can use both, without manipulation, by surfacing small early agreements and by showing that respected colleagues are already on board. When three of five leaders have signed off on an approach, the remaining two face a very different decision.

Unity. Cialdini's seventh principle is the pull of shared identity, the sense of being on the same team. A chief of staff who frames work around a common goal, "we are trying to hit the plan," rather than around the principal's wishes, taps a stronger motivation than any directive could.

The credibility that makes influence durable

Tactics get you a single yes. What gets you cooperation over months is a reputation. Operators who influence well over time tend to share a few habits.

They keep their word on small things, because reliability on small commitments is what earns latitude on large ones. They are discreet, because people share more with those who do not leak. They give credit outward and absorb blame inward, which makes colleagues want to work with them again. And they are honest about tradeoffs rather than overselling, because a reputation for straight talk is worth more than any single win.

There is a failure mode worth naming. An operator who leans on proximity to the principal, who signals "I speak for the boss" to force compliance, may win in the moment and lose the relationship. Borrowed authority spends down fast, and the resentment it creates outlasts the task. The stronger move is to make the request stand on its own merits and its fairness.

FAQ

Is influence without authority just a polite word for manipulation? No, when it is done well. Manipulation gets someone to act against their interest through deception. Influence, in the Cohen and Bradford sense, is finding a genuine exchange where both sides come out ahead. The test is whether the other person would still agree if they saw your full reasoning.

What if I offer real value and someone still refuses to cooperate? Then you escalate the decision, not the pressure. Part of the operator's job is knowing which conflicts you resolve laterally and which ones belong with the principal or the leadership team. Trying to force a peer who outranks your influence usually costs more than it returns.

How do I build influence when I am brand new and no one knows me? Start by being useful before you ask for anything. Learn people's worlds, unblock small things, and deliver on every commitment. Cialdini's reciprocity and authority principles both compound quietly over the first few months.

Does this change when I do have some formal authority? The tools still matter more than the title. Even leaders with direct reports get better results through influence than through orders, because willing cooperation outperforms grudging compliance. Authority is a backstop, not a first move.
