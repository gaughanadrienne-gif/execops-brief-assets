Takeaway: An executive assistant to a CEO protects the single scarcest resource in a company, the chief executive's time and attention, and the modern version of the job has grown from calendar keeper to a trusted operator who filters information, manages stakeholders, and keeps the office of the CEO running.

The executive assistant role is often described as if it stopped evolving in 1995. The picture people carry is a person answering phones and booking flights. That version still exists in some places, but the role at the top of a company looks very different now. An executive assistant to a CEO sits at the center of the information flow for the whole organization, and the job is defined by judgment as much as by logistics.

This is a plain breakdown of what the role actually involves today, what separates a strong CEO-level EA from a general administrative one, and where the job is heading.

The core of the job

Strip away the variation across companies and the role comes down to one thing: making the chief executive measurably more effective than they would be working alone.

Melba Duncan made the economic case for this plainly in Harvard Business Review. She noted that for the position to break even, an assistant only needs to make the executive about 8 percent more productive, which for a leader working a 60-hour week is roughly five hours saved. A skilled assistant to a senior leader saves far more than that. The math is why the role exists at all: at the CEO level, an hour of the principal's attention is worth a great deal, and someone has to guard it.

That framing matters because it reorders the priorities. The point of the job is not to complete a list of tasks. The point is to remove friction, drag, and low-value demands from the CEO's day so the highest-value work can happen.

What the work looks like week to week

The specific duties shift with the company and the executive, but most CEO-level EAs spend their time across a familiar set of responsibilities.

Owning the calendar as a strategic tool

At this level, the calendar is not a scheduling chore. It is a statement of priorities. A strong EA treats every request for the CEO's time as a question of trade-offs: what is this worth relative to everything else competing for the same hour. That means protecting focus blocks, building in buffer time so one overrun does not collapse the rest of the day, weighing which relationships require the principal in person, and saying no on the CEO's behalf far more often than saying yes.

Managing the flow of information

A chief executive is a target for an enormous volume of email, requests, updates, and asks. An experienced EA acts as a filter, deciding what reaches the principal now, what can be batched, what someone else should handle, and what needs a response drafted and ready for approval. Done well, this is one of the highest-leverage parts of the role, because it determines what the CEO even sees.

Coordinating complex travel and logistics

Executive travel is rarely simple. It involves time zones, back-to-back external meetings, contingencies when a flight slips, and the reality that a canceled connection can unravel a week of commitments. Handling this reliably, and quietly fixing it when it breaks, is a baseline expectation of the role.

Acting as a communication hub and gatekeeper

The EA is often the first point of contact for board members, senior leaders, investors, and important external partners. How the assistant handles those interactions shapes how the outside world experiences the office of the CEO. This requires discretion, polish, and a clear sense of what the principal would want in each case.

Preparing the principal

Increasingly, CEO-level EAs prepare the executive for what is coming: pulling together background before a meeting, flagging what to be aware of, making sure the right documents are ready, and confirming the principal walks in prepared rather than reacting on the fly.

How the modern role has changed

The traditional secretarial framing has narrowed over time, and the strategic partnership framing has expanded. Several forces pushed this shift.

Basic scheduling and communication tools automated the most routine parts of the old job, which raised the bar for what a person in the seat is expected to add. The U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics reflects this in its outlook: employment of secretaries and administrative assistants overall is projected to decline, even as executive-level support remains valued, because the commodity version of the work is easier to automate or absorb (BLS Occupational Outlook Handbook). The roles that remain and grow are the senior, judgment-heavy ones.

At the same time, the expectations attached to the top EA seat rose. Leaders increasingly want a thinking partner who understands the business, knows the stakeholders, and can represent the principal's interests across a range of situations. That is a meaningfully different job from taking dictation, and it is why the strongest executive assistants are treated as core members of the leadership operation rather than support staff.

Where the EA role sits next to the chief of staff

Because both roles serve a principal closely, they get confused. The clearest distinction comes from Dan Ciampa's HBR framing of the chief of staff. Ciampa describes the chief of staff as working autonomously and specifically not handling routine correspondence or the day-to-day calendar, because that work belongs to the executive assistant. The chief of staff operates a level up, on the leader's agenda and priorities across the organization.

Put simply, the EA keeps the principal's day and office working. The chief of staff keeps the principal's priorities working across the company. Many organizations run both roles side by side, and they complement each other rather than compete. That relationship also makes the EA seat one of the most natural launch points into a chief of staff role for people who want to move from running the office to shaping the agenda.

What it pays

Compensation varies widely by industry, company size, and location, and the CEO level sits at the top of the range for the profession. For a reference point, the Bureau of Labor Statistics reported a median annual wage of $73,680 for executive secretaries and executive administrative assistants in its May 2023 data (BLS OEWS). Assistants to chief executives at large or well-funded companies frequently earn well above that median, because the stakes attached to the seat are higher and the trust required is greater. Treat any single average with caution and evaluate each role on its own scope and package.

What separates a strong CEO-level EA

Three qualities tend to distinguish the people who thrive in the seat.

  1. Judgment under ambiguity. The best EAs make good calls without asking, because they understand the principal's priorities well enough to act as an extension of them.
  2. Discretion as a habit. The role carries constant exposure to sensitive information. Trust is the entire foundation, and it is easy to lose and hard to rebuild.
  3. Anticipation. Handling what is asked is table stakes. Seeing what will be needed before it is requested is what makes the role indispensable.

FAQ

Is an executive assistant to a CEO an administrative role or a strategic one? Both, and the balance has shifted toward the strategic over time. The role still owns real logistics, but at the CEO level the value comes from judgment, information filtering, and stakeholder management, not just task execution.

How is an EA to a CEO different from a chief of staff? The EA owns the principal's time, communications, and office operations. The chief of staff owns the principal's priorities and follow-through across the whole organization and operates a level up, without handling the day-to-day calendar (HBR).

Do you need a degree to be an executive assistant to a CEO? Usually not a specific one. Employers weigh demonstrated reliability, judgment, discretion, and a track record supporting senior leaders more heavily than a particular credential.

Is the executive assistant role disappearing because of automation? The commodity version of administrative support is shrinking, which the BLS outlook reflects, but senior executive support remains valued precisely because the judgment involved is hard to automate (BLS).

Can an executive assistant become a chief of staff? Yes, and it is one of the most common paths in. An EA already sees how the principal makes decisions and where the fires start, which is a strong foundation for stepping up into an agenda-owning role.
