Takeaway: Most chiefs of staff fall into one of three types, the operator, the strategist, and the proxy, and hiring for the wrong one is the most common way the role fails.

"Chief of staff" is not one job. Two people with the same title can spend their weeks on almost entirely different work, because the role bends to what a specific leader needs at a specific moment. That is why so many chief of staff hires disappoint. A company brings on a brilliant strategist when what it needed was an operator, or asks a process-minded operator to be the CEO's proxy in rooms they were never set up to command.

The way to avoid that mismatch is to name the type before you hire or apply. Here are the three main types, the archetype models that back them up, and how to pick.

Why the role splits into types

The chief of staff role is defined by the leader it serves more than by a standard job description. That flexibility is the whole point of the role, and it is also why it resists a single definition. Dan Ciampa's Harvard Business Review framing lists five functions a chief of staff performs, from air traffic controller to honest broker to confidant. Different leaders lean on different functions, and the emphasis shapes the type.

Several models exist to describe the variation. The Vannin Chief of Staff framework distills it to three archetypes: the operator, the strategist, and the proxy. Bain Capital Ventures uses four archetypes tied to company stage. The Chief of Staff Association has published a broader model of six modalities. The frameworks differ in count, but they agree on the core insight: the right type depends on the company's stage and the leader's gap.

The three types below map to the most durable version of that split.

Type 1: The operator

What they do. The operator is process-driven and detail-oriented, a person who turns strategy into execution. They build the operating rhythm, run leadership meetings, track decisions to completion, and impose structure where things are chaotic. The Vannin framework describes this archetype as acting like a junior COO, bringing repeatable process and clarity to an organization.

When a company needs one. When execution is the bottleneck. The strategy is clear enough, but priorities lack owners, meetings are unproductive, and follow-through is inconsistent. Fast-growing companies often need an operator first, because growth breaks the informal systems that worked when the team was small.

Signs of the fit. The leader keeps saying "we know what to do, we just cannot make it stick." Deadlines slip. Nobody owns the seams between teams. An operator closes those gaps.

Type 2: The strategist

What they do. The strategist combines operating competence with big-picture thinking. They work forward, helping the leader navigate growth, market shifts, and hard bets. Where the operator makes today's plan run, the strategist helps shape what the plan should be, framing decisions, running analysis, and pressure-testing direction (Vannin).

When a company needs one. When the leader's scarcest resource is thinking time on the biggest questions. This type fits organizations facing a strategic inflection, a new market, a major pivot, a fundraise, where the leader needs a thought partner who can go deep on the substance, not just keep the trains running.

Signs of the fit. The leader is buried in operational detail and cannot get to the strategic work only they can do. The big questions keep getting deferred. A strategist creates the space and does the groundwork so those decisions actually get made.

Type 3: The proxy

What they do. The proxy is the leader's trusted stand-in, someone who can represent the CEO across the organization and, at times, externally. They carry the leader's context and judgment closely enough to speak for them and to step in when the leader cannot be everywhere (Vannin). This is the most senior and trust-intensive of the three, and it usually develops over time rather than on day one.

When a company needs one. When the leader is stretched across so many demands that they need someone to extend their reach and judgment. Bain Capital Ventures describes a comparable "CEO brain extension" archetype for later-stage companies, where the CEO's time is dominated by external stakeholders and they need a trusted proxy to hold the internal fabric together. That model notes the type calls for the most experience, on the order of ten or more years.

Signs of the fit. The leader is the single point of failure for too many decisions. Their calendar is the company's constraint. A proxy absorbs some of that load by making calls the leader would make.

Matching the type to company stage

The archetypes are not just personality flavors. They track with what a company needs as it grows. Adapting the Bain Capital Ventures stage model:

  • Early stage, still finding product-market fit: a generalist operator who can cover gaps and bring order while founders focus on the core product.
  • Scaling, strategy set but execution straining: an operator or an operator-strategist blend to run cross-functional execution.
  • Growth stage with executive gaps: a strategist who can own hard questions and even stand in for unfilled leadership seats.
  • Late stage, CEO focused outward: a proxy who extends the CEO's judgment across the organization.

The experience the role requires rises with the type. An early operator role can suit someone a few years into their career. A proxy role generally calls for a decade or more, which matches McKinsey's finding that the average person entering a chief of staff role brings just over 12 years of experience (McKinsey).

How to pick the right type

For a company or leader hiring the role, work backward from the gap:

  1. Name the bottleneck. Is it execution, strategy, or the leader's own bandwidth? The answer points to operator, strategist, or proxy.
  2. Match seniority to the type. A proxy needs the experience to be credible speaking for the leader. An operator can be earlier in their career. Do not ask someone to play above the trust they have earned.
  3. Write the job around the type. Vague chief of staff job posts produce mismatches. Describe the actual work and the outcomes you expect.
  4. Expect the type to evolve. Many chiefs of staff start as operators and grow into strategist or proxy work as trust builds. The role is often a two to three year phase, so plan for it to change over that window (McKinsey).

For someone pursuing the role, the same map tells you where you fit today and where you want to grow. Most people build credibility as operators first, then earn the strategist and proxy scope over time.

FAQ

Are these the only frameworks for chief of staff types? No. The three-type model (operator, strategist, proxy) is a clean summary, but Bain Capital Ventures uses four stage-based archetypes, and the Chief of Staff Association has published a six-modality model. They describe the same underlying variation at different resolutions.

Can one chief of staff be all three types? Over time, often yes. Many people start as operators and grow into strategist and proxy work as trust deepens. At any given moment, though, one type usually dominates based on what the leader needs most.

Which type is most senior? The proxy, generally, because representing the leader requires the most trust and experience. The operator role can suit someone earlier in their career.

How do I know which type a job posting is really for? Read past the title to the outcomes. If the post is about process, meetings, and execution, it is an operator role. If it emphasizes strategy and analysis, it leans strategist. If it talks about representing the CEO and making decisions on their behalf, it is a proxy role.

Does the type change how long someone stays? It can. Operator and strategist stints are often the two to three year springboard McKinsey describes. Proxy relationships, built on deep trust, sometimes last longer because they are harder to rebuild with a new person.
