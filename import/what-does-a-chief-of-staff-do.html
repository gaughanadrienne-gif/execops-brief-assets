Takeaway: A chief of staff exists to make one leader and their leadership team more effective, by owning the priorities, information, and follow-through that would otherwise fall through the cracks.

Few roles are described more vaguely than chief of staff. Job posts range from glorified scheduler to shadow COO, and the same title can mean very different things at two companies down the street from each other. That ambiguity is not an accident. The role is defined by the needs of a specific leader more than by a fixed set of duties. Still, there is a clear center to the job once you strip away the noise.

This is a plain breakdown of what a chief of staff actually does, where the role came from, and how to tell whether a given "chief of staff" job is the real thing or a title upgrade with no substance.

The one-sentence definition

A chief of staff is a senior operator who works directly for a principal, usually a CEO or another top executive, and is responsible for the smooth running of that leader's priorities and leadership team.

The role is not primarily administrative. As Dan Ciampa put it in Harvard Business Review, a chief of staff works autonomously and does not handle routine correspondence or manage the leader's day-to-day calendar. That work belongs to an executive assistant. The chief of staff operates a level up, on the leader's agenda rather than their inbox.

Where the role came from

The title borrows directly from the military and government. In a military headquarters, the chief of staff coordinates the staff functions so the commander can focus on command. The White House adapted the same idea. The modern White House staff traces to 1939, when the Executive Office of the President was created, and John Steelman became the first person to hold a chief of staff style role under President Truman in 1946. The job took on its recognizable modern shape when Sherman Adams served under President Eisenhower starting in 1953, controlling access, managing the staff, and shielding the president's time (Wikipedia).

Business borrowed the concept later, and adoption accelerated as companies grew more complex and CEOs found they needed more strategic support than an assistant could provide. The role now has its own professional infrastructure, including the Chief of Staff Association, which has partnered with institutions such as Harvard Business School's executive education arm to train people in it (HBS Executive Education).

The five functions at the core of the job

Ciampa's HBR framing is the most useful shorthand for what the work involves. A strong chief of staff plays five roles at once.

1. Air traffic controller

The chief of staff manages the flow of decisions, meetings, and requests reaching the leader and the senior team. That means deciding what deserves the principal's attention now, what can wait, and what someone else should handle. When too many priorities compete for the same limited hours, the chief of staff sequences them.

2. Integrator

Large organizations silo naturally. Work streams that should connect often do not, because no single executive owns the seam between two departments. The chief of staff connects those threads, making sure a decision in one function does not quietly break something in another.

3. Communicator

The chief of staff links the leadership team to the rest of the organization, translating strategy into terms teams can act on and carrying real signal back up. A decision is only as good as the follow-through, and the chief of staff often owns the communication that turns a decision into action.

4. Honest broker

A good chief of staff can give the leader a wide, unbiased read on an issue, free of the turf interests that shape what other executives say. Because the role has no functional empire to defend, the chief of staff can name the real problem when others will not.

5. Confidant

The role sits close to the leader and carries sensitive context. Trust is the currency. A chief of staff hears unfinished thinking, disagreements, and doubts, and is expected to be discreet with all of it.

What the work looks like in practice

The specific tasks vary, but most chiefs of staff spend their weeks on some mix of the following:

  • Running the leadership operating rhythm. Owning the cadence of leadership meetings, setting agendas, capturing decisions, and tracking follow-ups so nothing is decided twice.
  • Driving priority projects. Taking on the two or three initiatives that matter most and do not have a clear owner, and pushing them to completion across teams.
  • Preparing the principal. Building the briefings, decision memos, and pre-reads a leader needs to walk into a meeting ready, instead of reacting on the fly.
  • Managing information flow. Building the single source of truth for goals, metrics, and decisions so the leadership team argues about the right things.
  • Acting as a sounding board. Pressure-testing ideas before they become directives, and flagging risks the leader cannot see from their seat.

McKinsey's analysis of roughly 250 chiefs of staff found the average person entering the role brings just over 12 years of experience and typically moves into it from a midlevel leadership position such as senior manager (McKinsey). This is not an entry-level job. It sits close enough to the top that the person needs enough seniority to be credible with executives.

What a chief of staff does not do

Clarity about the boundaries matters as much as the duties.

  • Not an executive assistant. The chief of staff does not live in the calendar and inbox. Many organizations run both roles side by side, and they complement each other.
  • Not a decision maker by title. The chief of staff influences decisions and often shapes them, but the authority stays with the principal. The role runs on borrowed authority and earned trust, not a formal mandate over other teams.
  • Not a permanent operating role. The job is intense and usually time-bound. McKinsey found a median tenure of about 2.3 years, and most people move up more than one level afterward, which tells you the role is often a springboard rather than a destination (McKinsey).

How to tell a real chief of staff role from a title

If you are evaluating a job, look for three signals:

  1. Direct access to the principal. A real chief of staff reports to and works alongside the leader, not three layers down.
  2. Ownership of priorities, not just logistics. The role should own outcomes on the leader's most important work, not only meeting logistics.
  3. A seat where decisions happen. The chief of staff is in the room where the leadership team sets direction.

When those three are missing, the title is usually a relabeled coordinator job. When they are present, you are looking at the real thing.

FAQ

Is a chief of staff a senior role? Usually yes. The people who take it on average bring more than a decade of experience, and the role sits close to the top of the organization (McKinsey). It is not a starter job, though the exact seniority varies with the company's size.

Who does a chief of staff report to? The principal they serve, most often the CEO, but sometimes another C-level executive such as a CTO or a division head.

Does a chief of staff have authority over other people? Rarely by title. The role usually carries little formal authority over other teams and works through influence, trust, and the principal's mandate instead.

How long do people stay in the role? Not long, by design. Median tenure is around 2.3 years, and most people use it as a stepping stone to a more senior position (McKinsey).

Is a chief of staff the same as a COO? No. A COO owns operations with formal authority and a permanent seat. A chief of staff supports a leader and works through influence. The two can exist at the same company.
