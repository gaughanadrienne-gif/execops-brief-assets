Takeaway: A chief of staff runs on the principal's trust, and the Trust Equation from The Trusted Advisor breaks that trust into four factors you can actually work on, three that build it and one that quietly destroys it.

Trust is the whole job

Most descriptions of the chief of staff role list tasks: run the meeting, build the plan, manage the priorities. But the tasks only get handed to you if one thing is in place first. The principal has to trust you with information they share with almost no one, with decisions that carry real weight, and with a view into how the organization actually works. Lose that trust and the role empties out overnight, no matter how good your execution.

This is why the operator's relationship with trust is different from most professionals'. For many roles, trust is helpful. For a chief of staff, it is the license to operate. Confidence, in both senses of the word, the principal's confidence in you and the confidential information you hold, is the currency the entire role trades in.

The useful thing is that trust is not a mystery. It has structure, and one framework maps it better than any other.

The Trust Equation

In their 2000 book The Trusted Advisor, David Maister, Charles Green, and Robert Galford proposed a way to break trustworthiness into components. They expressed it as an equation (Trusted Advisor Associates):

Trust = (Credibility + Reliability + Intimacy) / Self-Orientation

Three factors in the numerator build trust. One factor in the denominator erodes it. The structure is the insight. It says trust is not a single quality you have or lack, but a balance of four things you can assess and improve one at a time.

Here is what each factor means, in the authors' framing.

Credibility is about words. Can you be believed? It comes from what you say and whether it holds up, from expertise, accuracy, and honesty about what you do and do not know.

Reliability is about actions. Do you do what you say you will do? It is built through repeated, dependable follow-through, the accumulated evidence that your commitments are real.

Intimacy is about safety. Do people feel safe being open with you? It comes from discretion and emotional closeness, the sense that what someone shares with you is held carefully and that you can be trusted with the difficult and the unfinished.

Self-orientation is the denominator, and it is the one that quietly determines everything. It measures where your focus sits: on yourself, or on the other person and the shared goal. High self-orientation, someone visibly working their own angle, destroys trust even when the other three factors are strong (Trusted Advisor Associates).

The authors note that of the four, credibility and reliability are the easiest to build and, on their own, the least powerful. Intimacy and especially self-orientation move trust the most (Trusted Advisor Associates). Most people over-invest in the first two, which are the visible, comfortable ones, and under-invest in the two that actually decide the outcome.

Reading the equation as an operator

Each factor points to a specific way a chief of staff earns or loses standing.

Credibility: know what you are talking about

An operator's credibility is not conferred by the title. It is earned by consistently understanding the business, the numbers, and the context better than the room expects. When you say a project is behind, the principal should be able to take it as fact. That reputation is built one accurate statement at a time and lost with a single confident claim that turns out to be wrong.

The discipline that protects credibility is saying "I don't know, I'll find out" instead of guessing. Cialdini's research on the authority principle points the same direction: perceived expertise and trustworthiness, not position, are what make people defer (Influence at Work). An operator builds that authority by being right, and by being honest about the edges of what they know.

Reliability: close every loop

Reliability is where operators either compound trust or bleed it, because the role is a stream of commitments. You said you would circle back on the hiring question. You said the deck would be ready Thursday. You said you would talk to the head of sales. Each of those is a small test, and the results add up into a reputation. An operator who closes every loop, even the small ones, becomes someone the principal stops worrying about, which is the point. Reliability is not glamorous. It is a track record, built in dozens of unremarkable follow-throughs.

Intimacy: be safe to talk to

For a chief of staff, intimacy is close to the whole job. The principal has to be able to think out loud with you, to voice a half-formed doubt about a strategy or a person, to be uncertain in front of you without it costing them. That only happens if you have proven you can hold what you are told. Discretion is the foundation. An operator who repeats a confidence, even once, even harmlessly, teaches the principal to filter what they share, and a filtered principal is one you can no longer fully serve.

Intimacy also means being willing to have the honest conversation. The trusted operator is the one person who will tell the principal a plan is weak or that they handled a meeting poorly, and who can do it in a way that lands as care rather than criticism. That willingness, paired with discretion, is what makes an operator genuinely valuable rather than merely pleasant.

Self-orientation: the factor that decides everything

The denominator is where operators quietly win or lose. A chief of staff sits close to power, sees everything, and has real influence, which creates constant temptation to work their own position. The moment a principal senses that you are managing up for your own advancement, protecting yourself in a decision, or shading information to look good, trust collapses. It does not matter how credible, reliable, and discreet you are. High self-orientation poisons the whole equation.

The defense is genuine, not performed. You lower self-orientation by actually caring more about the principal's success and the organization's goals than about your own standing, and by making that visible in the small moments: giving credit away, raising a risk that reflects badly on you, advocating for the right answer even when it is not the convenient one. Operators who get this right find that their own standing rises anyway, precisely because they were not chasing it. Operators who get it wrong can execute flawlessly and still never be trusted with anything that matters.

Building it, and rebuilding it

Trust builds slowly and breaks fast, which means the equation is less a one-time achievement than a daily practice. A few principles help.

Work the whole equation, not just the comfortable half. If you are strong on credibility and reliability but the relationship still feels transactional, the gap is almost always intimacy or self-orientation. That is the harder work, and the work that pays.

Guard the denominator above all. One visible act of self-interest can undo months of dependable delivery. When you are unsure whether a choice reads as self-serving, assume it does and act accordingly.

And if trust breaks, name it and repair it directly. A missed commitment or a leaked confidence is not always fatal, but it is only recoverable through honest acknowledgment and a sustained return to reliability and discretion. Pretending it did not happen guarantees it stays broken.

FAQ

Who created the Trust Equation? David Maister, Charles Green, and Robert Galford, in their 2000 book The Trusted Advisor. The framework is Trust = (Credibility + Reliability + Intimacy) / Self-Orientation (Trusted Advisor Associates).

Which factor matters most? Self-orientation, in the denominator. The authors point out that intimacy and self-orientation move trust the most, while credibility and reliability, though easier to build, do the least on their own. High self-orientation undermines everything else.

How is intimacy different from just being liked? Being liked is pleasant; intimacy is safety. It is the confidence that someone can be open and even vulnerable with you and that what they share will be held carefully. For an operator, that rests on proven discretion, not charm.

Can trust be rebuilt after it breaks? Sometimes, and only through direct acknowledgment followed by a sustained return to reliability and discretion. Small breaches are usually recoverable; a serious breach of confidence rarely is, which is why the denominator deserves the most protection.
