Takeaway: Most people get their first chief of staff role by doing the work before the title exists and getting promoted from inside a company that already trusts them, not by applying cold to a job post, so the winning strategy is to build proof and relationships where you already are.

The chief of staff job creates a chicken-and-egg problem. Postings want someone who has done it, but you cannot have done it without a first role. Applying to external listings as an unknown quantity is the hardest possible path, and the one most first-timers default to. There is a better route, and the data points straight at it.

This is a practical guide to landing the first role without the title, built around how these jobs actually get filled.

The most reliable path is internal

Start with the single most useful fact about hiring for this role. McKinsey's study of roughly 250 chiefs of staff found that nearly two-thirds are appointed from within their organizations, and at companies with 50,000 or more employees, 80 percent are internal hires (McKinsey). Principals value deep institutional knowledge and a trusted internal network, because the role runs on trust and context that an outsider cannot bring on day one.

The takeaway for you is direct. Your best shot at a first chief of staff role is usually inside the company you already work for, with a leader who already knows you. That reframes the whole effort. Instead of competing as a stranger against experienced external candidates, you compete as a known quantity solving a problem the leader can already feel.

Do the job before you have the title

The most durable way in is to expand your current role until a chief of staff title simply formalizes what you already do. This works because it replaces the thing you lack, a track record in the role, with the thing that actually gets people hired, evidence.

Look for work that mirrors the core of the job and volunteer for it.

  • Own a cross-functional problem nobody has. Take the messy initiative that spans two departments and has no clear owner, and drive it to completion. This is the integrator function Ciampa describes, and it is the most visible proof you can produce (HBR).
  • Improve how the leadership team runs. Offer to tighten the meeting cadence, capture decisions, and track follow-ups so nothing is decided twice. Owning even part of the operating rhythm puts you close to where decisions happen.
  • Prepare a leader for what is coming. Start producing short briefs before key meetings instead of just logistics. It demonstrates judgment about what matters, not just diligence.
  • Become the reliable connector. Build the working relationships across functions that let you move information and unblock work. Much of the job is influence without authority, and that credibility takes time to build.

Every one of these produces a result you can point to later. When the conversation about a formal role happens, you want to show outcomes, not potential.

Propose the role if it does not exist

Many leaders would benefit from a chief of staff and have simply never framed their problem that way. That is an opening. The Chief of Staff Network makes this point directly: there are many principals who would benefit from the role but are unfamiliar with it, and part of getting in is showing a specific leader how you would solve their problems by taking it on (Chief of Staff Network).

Make it concrete. Write a short proposal that names the specific problems a chief of staff would solve for your principal, defines the scope, shows where the role sits on the org chart, and sets clear expectations at 30, 60, and 90 days. Leaders respond far better to a specific, well-reasoned proposal than to a general request for a bigger job. You are not asking for a promotion. You are diagnosing a problem the leader already has and offering a defined solution with your name on it.

Close the credibility gaps deliberately

If you are moving from a support or specialist background, the most common gap is business fluency. Ciampa lists business savvy among the foundational abilities an effective chief of staff needs, alongside project management, communication, and a feel for the pressures facing the leader (HBR). You do not need an MBA to close this. You need to understand how your company actually makes money, which operating metrics leadership watches, how the budget cycle works, and how decisions get funded. Read the board deck if you can get it. Sit in on the reviews. The goal is to follow the substance of a leadership conversation, not just the logistics around it.

Then pick your two or three real gaps, honestly, from the recurring requirements in chief of staff postings in your industry, strategic planning, program management, data literacy, financial acumen, executive communication, and close them through stretch assignments, a targeted course, or mentorship from a current chief of staff. Deliberate, visible skill-building beats a general claim that you are ready.

When you do go external, target the beatable roles

Sometimes the right move is a new company, especially if your current employer has no path to the role. The internal-hire data tells you where to aim. Since the largest, most established companies fill the role internally 80 percent of the time, those are the hardest external targets (McKinsey). Earlier-stage and fast-scaling companies are more open to strong outside operators who can start building immediately, because they have less internal bench and a more urgent, gap-filling version of the role.

A few tactics raise your odds:

  • Look beyond the exact title. Roles labeled business operations, strategy and operations, or special projects to the CEO are often the same job under a different name, and they draw less competition from people fixated on the chief of staff label.
  • Lead with proof, not aspiration. Frame your background around cross-functional results you have driven, not around wanting the role. Hiring principals are testing whether you can get hard, ambiguous things done.
  • Work your network, because these jobs travel through it. Chief of staff roles are frequently filled through relationships and referrals rather than open postings. Connect with people in the role, attend the places they gather, and make your interest known before a job is posted.

Avoid the common first-timer mistakes

Three errors sink otherwise strong candidates. The first is waiting to be chosen, when the role almost always goes to the person who creates the opening. The second is leading with opinions instead of execution, when early credibility comes from getting hard things done, not from having views in meetings. The third is treating an internal move casually, when you should prepare for it as seriously as you would an external interview, because the bar for someone the leader will trust with sensitive work is high.

FAQ

Can you become a chief of staff without ever holding the title? Yes, and it is the norm. Nearly two-thirds of chiefs of staff are internal appointments, and most first-timers earn the role by doing the work and getting promoted rather than by being hired cold with prior title experience (McKinsey).

Do you need an MBA to become a chief of staff? No. Business fluency matters, but it can be built by understanding your company's economics and metrics rather than through a specific degree. Demonstrated judgment and cross-functional results carry more weight (HBR).

What if my company does not have a chief of staff role? Propose it. Write a short document naming the problems the role would solve for your leader, its scope, and 30, 60, and 90-day expectations. Many leaders benefit from the role but have never named it (Chief of Staff Network).

Is it easier to get a first chief of staff role at a startup or a big company? Startups and fast-scaling companies are usually more open to outside first-timers, since large companies fill the role internally about 80 percent of the time (McKinsey).

What experience should I highlight if I have never held the title? Cross-functional projects you have driven to completion, any ownership of a leadership operating rhythm, and evidence of influence without authority. Those map to the core of the role better than any single credential.
