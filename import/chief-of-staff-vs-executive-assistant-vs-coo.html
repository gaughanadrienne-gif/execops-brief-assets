Takeaway: An executive assistant runs the leader's day, a chief of staff runs the leader's priorities, and a COO runs the company's operations, and confusing the three is how companies end up with the wrong hire.

These three roles get blended constantly, in job posts and in casual conversation. All three sit close to a senior leader. All three make that leader more effective. But they operate at different altitudes, carry different authority, and solve different problems. Hiring one when you needed another is a common and expensive mistake.

Here is how the roles actually differ, with the boundaries drawn clearly.

The quick version

  • Executive assistant (EA): owns the leader's time and logistics. Calendar, inbox, travel, meeting prep, and the daily flow of the office.
  • Chief of staff (CoS): owns the leader's priorities and leadership rhythm. Strategic projects, decision follow-through, information flow, and cross-team coordination.
  • Chief operating officer (COO): owns the company's operations. A formal executive with authority over functions, budgets, and people.

The simplest way to keep them straight: the EA manages the day, the chief of staff manages the agenda, and the COO manages the machine.

Executive assistant: master of the day

The executive assistant keeps the leader's working life running. That means owning the calendar, triaging the inbox, arranging travel, coordinating meetings, and handling the steady stream of logistics that would otherwise eat a leader's hours. A great EA protects time and reduces friction, and the best ones anticipate needs before they are voiced.

The role is logistical and relational rather than strategic by design. That is a feature, not a limitation. An executive with a strong EA gets hours back every week and rarely drops a commitment.

The distinction from a chief of staff is explicit in the research. Writing in Harvard Business Review, Dan Ciampa noted that a chief of staff works autonomously and, unlike an executive assistant, does not handle routine correspondence or manage the leader's day-to-day schedule. The two roles are complementary, not competing. Many leaders have both, and they divide the work cleanly: the EA owns the day, the chief of staff owns the priorities.

Chief of staff: master of the agenda

The chief of staff operates one level up from logistics, on the substance of what the leader is trying to accomplish. The job centers on priorities, decisions, and follow-through: running the leadership operating rhythm, driving the initiatives that lack a clear owner, preparing the leader for the decisions that matter, and making sure information moves cleanly up and down.

Ciampa's HBR framing captures the range. A chief of staff acts as an air traffic controller for the leader and senior team, an integrator connecting siloed work streams, a communicator between leadership and the wider organization, an honest broker who can give an unbiased read, and a confidant.

The defining feature is that most of this authority is borrowed. A chief of staff typically has little formal command over other teams and works through the leader's mandate, trust, and influence. That is a real difference from a COO, who holds line authority.

The role also tends to be a phase rather than a career. McKinsey's study of roughly 250 chiefs of staff found a median tenure around 2.3 years, with most people moving up more than one level afterward (McKinsey). People take the job to get close to how top decisions get made, then move on to run something themselves.

COO: master of the machine

The chief operating officer is a formal C-suite executive responsible for the daily operations of the business. The COO is usually second in command after the CEO, reports directly to them, and can act on their behalf. The job carries real authority over people, budgets, and functions, and typically covers areas such as operations, supply chain, and other core execution functions (Corporate Finance Institute).

The COO role is famously variable. In their HBR study of the position, Nathan Bennett and Stephen Miles found that a COO's job tends to be defined in relation to the specific CEO they serve, more than by a fixed set of duties common across companies. That variability is one reason people confuse the COO with the chief of staff, since both are shaped by the leader they support.

The difference that settles it is authority and permanence. A COO owns operations with a formal mandate and a lasting seat in the executive team. A chief of staff influences and coordinates without that line authority, and usually for a shorter run.

Where the confusion comes from

Three things blur these roles.

They all serve the leader. Proximity to a CEO makes any support role look similar from the outside. The difference is what each one owns.

Titles get inflated. A senior EA is sometimes retitled chief of staff without the scope changing, and a chief of staff is sometimes called a COO to signal seniority. Read the responsibilities, not the label.

Scope shifts by company size. In a small startup, one person may cover pieces of all three. In a large company, the roles separate cleanly. The blurriest cases live in the middle, at companies growing fast enough that the org chart has not caught up to reality.

How to know which one you need

If your problem is that the leader's day is chaotic and commitments slip, you need an executive assistant.

If your problem is that priorities lack owners, decisions do not turn into action, and the leadership team keeps relitigating the same questions, you need a chief of staff.

If your problem is that the company's operations need a senior owner with real authority over functions and budgets, you need a COO.

The mistake to avoid is hiring a chief of staff to do a COO's job, or expecting an EA to carry strategic ownership. Each role solves a different problem, and the fix starts with naming the problem correctly.

FAQ

Can one person be both a chief of staff and an executive assistant? At small companies, sometimes. As the organization grows, the roles usually separate, because owning the calendar and owning strategic priorities pull in different directions. Many leaders run both roles in parallel.

Is a chief of staff more senior than an executive assistant? They operate at different altitudes rather than on a single ladder. A chief of staff typically carries more strategic scope and enters the role with more than a decade of experience (McKinsey), while a great EA is a deep specialist in a different discipline.

Is a chief of staff a step below the COO? Not exactly. A COO holds formal authority over operations, while a chief of staff works largely through influence. Some chiefs of staff do move into COO or general management roles later, but the jobs are different in kind, not just in rank.

Do you need a COO if you have a chief of staff? They solve different problems, so many companies have both. A chief of staff cannot replace the formal operating authority of a COO, and a COO does not usually own the CEO's personal operating rhythm the way a chief of staff does.

Which role should a growing startup hire first? It depends on the bottleneck. Founders drowning in logistics often start with an EA. Founders whose priorities keep slipping and whose leadership meetings feel unproductive often need a chief of staff before a full COO.
